My Research

I study how people interact with AI systems that feel conversational, supportive, and socially present. My work is grounded in Human-Computer Interaction, accessibility, and Human-AI Interaction, with a current focus on older adults’ experiences with generative AI chatbots.

Recently, I have been thinking a lot about AI sycophancy: the tendency of AI systems to agree with, flatter, or overly validate users. This issue is easy to describe in extreme cases, such as when a chatbot clearly tells a user that they are right even when they are not. But I am more interested in the less obvious cases. What happens when a chatbot gives a response that sounds neutral, gentle, or emotionally supportive, but the user still reads it as agreement?

This question feels important because many people turn to AI not just for facts, but also for advice, reassurance, companionship, or self-reflection. In these moments, a response that feels warm and validating can be very appealing. At the same time, too much agreement may reduce reflection, reinforce existing assumptions, or make the system seem more trustworthy than it actually is.

I am particularly interested in older adults because their interactions with AI are often discussed through broad ideas such as adoption, usability, or digital literacy. I want to look more closely at how older adults evaluate the quality of AI conversations: what feels meaningful, what feels trustworthy, what feels too shallow, and what kinds of support they actually want from these systems.

My goal is to contribute to the design of AI systems that can be supportive without being blindly agreeable. I hope this work can help us think more carefully about what responsible AI companionship should look like, especially for people who may use these systems for reflection, emotional support, or everyday social connection.
